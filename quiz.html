Multiple Choice
What is the key difference between chronic and acute food insecurity?
A
Chronic affects adults while acute affects children
B
Chronic is long-term and rooted in structural problems; acute is sudden and triggered by emergencies
C
Acute food insecurity only happens in developing countries
D
Chronic food insecurity is always more dangerous than acute food insecurity
✓ Correct! Chronic food insecurity builds over a long period due to persistent poverty and inequality, while acute food insecurity hits suddenly — often due to war, natural disasters, or economic shocks — and demands urgent humanitarian response.
2
Multiple Choice
Which of the following is the most direct cause of chronic food insecurity?
A
Earthquakes and floods
B
Currency devaluation
C
Poverty — people cannot consistently afford enough nutritious food
D
War and civil conflict
✓ Correct! Poverty is the most direct and persistent cause of chronic food insecurity. Low income limits access to food, transportation, and cooking facilities — and keeps people trapped in a cycle of hunger that doesn't go away on its own.
3
Multiple Choice
A person eats plenty of cheap processed food every day and feels full — but their diet lacks vitamins and minerals. Which term best describes their situation?
A
Hunger — they are physically starving
B
Undernutrition — they are not getting enough calories
C
Malnutrition — their diet is nutritionally unbalanced despite feeling full
D
Overnutrition — they are consuming too many calories only
✓ Correct! Malnutrition refers to any imbalance in nutrition — including eating too much of the wrong things. You can be malnourished without ever feeling hungry, which is why it's important to distinguish it from hunger.
4
Multiple Choice
Approximately what fraction of all food produced globally is wasted every year?
A
One tenth (10%)
B
One quarter (25%)
C
One third (roughly 33%)
D
One half (50%)
✓ Correct! About one third of all food produced globally is wasted every year — a staggering amount. Reducing food waste is one of the most powerful levers we have to improve food security without growing more food.
5
Multiple Choice
Which region has the highest rate of food insecurity — nearly double the global average?
A
South Asia
B
Latin America
C
Africa — approximately 58% face moderate or severe food insecurity
D
Eastern Europe
✓ Correct! Africa has the highest prevalence of food insecurity at around 58% — nearly double the global rate of approximately 30%. This is driven by a combination of poverty, conflict, climate vulnerability, and structural inequality.
6
Multiple Choice
A farming community loses its entire harvest due to a severe drought. The following year, drought strikes again, and the year after. What best describes their situation over time?
A
Only acute food insecurity — each drought is a separate emergency
B
Only chronic food insecurity — droughts are not sudden events
C
Both — recurring acute crises can transition a community into chronic food insecurity
D
Neither — food insecurity only applies to urban populations
✓ Correct! Both types can happen to the same people. A single drought is an acute crisis, but repeated droughts — especially driven by climate change — slide a community into chronic food insecurity. The two types are not mutually exclusive.
7
Multiple Choice
When local markets are still functioning during a crisis, which emergency response strategy is often considered the most effective and dignified?
A
Airlifting pre-packaged food rations from abroad
B
Cash or voucher transfers — letting people buy food from local markets themselves
C
Building new roads in the affected region immediately
D
Waiting for the government to restore food supplies naturally
✓ Correct! Cash or voucher transfers are often faster, more flexible, and more dignified than direct food aid — they allow people to choose what to buy, support local economies, and reduce logistical costs. They work best when local markets are still functioning.
8
Multiple Choice
Which of these statements about food insecurity, hunger, and malnutrition is true?
A
Hunger and malnutrition are exactly the same thing
B
Food insecurity only affects people who feel physically hungry
C
Malnutrition only occurs when someone has too little food
D
You can be food insecure without being hungry, and malnourished without feeling hungry
✓ Correct! These three concepts are distinct. Food insecurity is about reliability of access — even someone who eats daily might be food insecure if they can't count on the next meal. Malnutrition includes overnutrition, so someone eating cheap processed food can be malnourished without ever feeling hungry.
